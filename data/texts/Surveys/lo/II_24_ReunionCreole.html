1.  Introduction[Note 1: I am grateful to Pierre Cellier and Aimeric Daval-Markussen for very useful comments on this article.]

Reunion Creole is spoken by the majority of the 800,000 inhabitants of Reunion Island, a French Overseas Department, and by a considerable number of diaspora speakers in metropolitan France. It is difficult to estimate the number of speakers because French statistics do not provide information on language use.

 

      

2.  Sociohistorical background[Note 2: Information is drawn from Chaudenson (1974: 452–465), Chaudenson (2001: 29–31), and Boll√©e (2007: 77–117).]

Reunion Creole is without doubt a true creole in the socio-historical sense of the term, but differs from other French-based creoles in some respects: The language forms a complex continuum from acrolect to basilect, with many intermediate varieties of remarkable inconsistency. Its grammar shows features of French which have been maintained in the process of creolization or were reintroduced later on. The linguistic peculiarity of Reunion Creole is, of course, a result of its history, but geographical factors also play a role, the mountainous nature of the island having caused very poor internal communications until the last century. Compared to the plantation societies in other French colonies, the situation in Bourbon (the island‚Äôs name from 1649 until 1848) has been described by historians as exceptional, the most important features being widespread interethnic marriage, maintenance of small-scale agriculture during the plantation period, and the emergence of a white proletariat.

France took possession of the uninhabited island in 1640, but permanent settlement and colonization did not start before 1665. Many of the first French settlers had Malagasy spouses and servants; others married young women (‚Äúm√©tisses indo-portugaises‚Äù) brought from India for this purpose. The first census, held in 1686, shows that interethnic marriages were frequent (Chaudenson 1974: 455).

Slavery was at first formally prohibited, but was gradually introduced de facto and was tolerated after the promulgation of the Code noir in the Caribbean in 1685; with certain modifications this code took effect in the Mascarenes in 1723. The slave trade, officially illegal until this date, began in 1697 with the arrival of 21 slaves from Africa and Madagascar. In the 17th century, the colony did not prosper and its population grew very slowly; it was not until after 1717 that the slaves began to outnumber the whites (see Table 1 for the population development).[Note 3: This term ‚Äúwhite‚Äù is somewhat misleading, since according to the census of 1709 only 169 out of 492 persons of the ‚Äúpopulation libre‚Äù were ‚Äúvrais Blancs‚Äù, i.e. European immigrants and their descendants (Boll√©e 2007: 83).] The demographic development in the decade 1725–1735 reflects the transition from a homestead society (‚Äúsoci√©t√© d‚Äôhabitation‚Äù) to a plantation society (‚Äúsoci√©t√© de plantation‚Äù), as described by Chaudenson (2001: 96–129); this development followed the introduction of coffee planting around 1725. However, even during the plantation period a large number of small farms existed, producing mainly foodstuffs. These foodstuffs also supplied Mauritius since, in the 18th century, Bourbon functioned as the ‚Äúgranary‚Äù of the Mascarenes. In 1795, as many as 1,850, i.e. two thirds of the owners, had 1 to 10 slaves, and many had no slaves at all. Two factors led to the gradual development of a white proletariat called ‚ÄúPetits Blancs‚Äù (poor whites) in the course of the 18th century: The growing number of new colonists after 1725 caused a shortage of land, and the French patterns of inheritance required the division of land among all the children, with dramatic results in large families. The landless whites tried to make a living in the island‚Äôs highlands where some remote places, even today, are only accessible on foot.

Madagascar remained the main source of slaves until the 1760s when the trade from East Africa became more important. The sugar industry did not develop until the first decade of the 19th century, paradoxically at the same time when the slave trade met with growing political resistence. It was completely prohibited in 1817 but could not be stopped: about 33,000 Africans (Cafres) were clandestinely imported until 1830 and 10,000 to 20,000 after 1830. The shortage of manual labour led to the immigration of free servants from 1825 onwards; indentured workers (engag√©s), mostly from Southern India (Malbars), arrived in larger numbers after the abolition of slavery in 1848. ‚ÄúIn 1887, of the 210,000 residents of the island, 80,000 were recent immigrants!‚Äù (Chaudenson 2001: 30). Among these recent immigrants were Chinese, who were recruited as field hands but quickly took over the grocery business so that today the word sinwa also means ‚Äògrocery‚Äô. The last immigrants to arrive towards the end of the 19th century were Muslim Indians from Gujarat, misnamed Zarab by the Creoles. Towards the end of the 20th century, whites made up about a quarter of the population and the Malbar a little less than a quarter, while Chinese numbered 15,000 and the Zarab 6,000; ‚Äúthe remaining R√©unionnais, about half of the total population, [were] of mixed and diverse ethnic origin‚Äù (Chaudenson 2001: 30).

The study of two religious texts by Philippe Caulier, probably written in the 1760s (Boll√©e 2007), supports Chaudenson‚Äôs hypothesis that a continuum of approximative varieties of French developed in the homestead society when the slaves were in close contact with the colonists and their families and that creolization in the strict sense began in the plantation society when large numbers of newly arrived slaves, without ready access to the model of Colonial French as spoken by the colonists, attempted to acquire these approximative varieties from the Creole slaves in charge of their acculturation. A comparison of the Caulier texts, the Fables of Louis H√©ry, published in 1828/1856 (Chaudenson 1981), and the recordings made for the Atlas linguistique et ethnographique de la R√©union (ALR) in the 1970s shows that the formation of Reunion Creole was a gradual process and that some phenomena of linguistic change have led to fluctuating results. Data for the present article are mainly taken from the recordings made for the ALR by Christian Barat, namely transcriptions in Cellier (1982), examples in Cellier (1985), examples and short texts printed in the ALR, and stories told by the informants who were interviewed for the ALR, published by Barat et al. in 1977.

3.  Sociolinguistic situation

The linguistic situation of Reunion has been described in terms of the Fergusonian diglossia insofar as bilingual speakers tend to use creole in private contexts and informal situations, and French in public contexts and formal situations (but not exclusively); part of the population, however, ‚Äúconsists of passive bilinguals (who understand French, more or less, but speak it hardly, if at all)‚Äù (Chaudenson 2001: 30). Since the beginning of the 21st century the ‚Äúclassic‚Äù diglossia with Creole as the ‚Äúlow variety‚Äù seems to be changing. ‚ÄúThe patient march of the creole languages‚Äú (‚ÄúLa patiente marche des langues cr√©oles‚Äù) – to quote the title of a colloquium organized in October 2008 by the Conseil R√©gional de la R√©union and the Office de la langue cr√©ole (founded in 2006) – has made some progress towards a ‚Äúbilinguisme fran√ßais-cr√©ole harmonieux‚Äù. On the occasion of this colloquium, Axel Gauvin, novelist and director of the Office, announced the results of a poll showing that ‚Äúla repr√©sentation du cr√©ole est positive‚Äù, creole language and culture being regarded as an essential ingredient of R√©unionnais identity[Note 5: Lofis La Lang Kr√©ol La R√©nion: Sondage IPSOS Mars 2007, 2 vol.].

     The creoles spoken in the French Overseas Departments Martinique, Guadeloupe, French Guiana, and Reunion were totally ignored by French legislation until recently, even though they are the most vital regional languages of France with the largest numbers of speakers; in 2000 they were finally given the official status of regional languages. The much debated creation of a creole certificate of aptitude for teaching in secondary education (CAPES) in 2002 has apparently had the positive effect that Creole now finds its place in the education system and that competent teachers take an active part in the ‚Äúpatient march of Creole‚Äù which will no doubt still have to overcome some obstacles, one of them being the lack of an officialized orthography – in the CAPES exams, students can choose from among four different spelling systems.

5.  Noun Phrase

The grammar of Reunion Creole is characterized by pervasive variation and apparently many variants are typical either of the basilect or of the acrolect (e.g. en zalimet/ eunn alumet ‚Äòa match‚Äô, mon fanm / ma fanm ‚Äòmy wife‚Äô). For a thorough description it would, therefore, be necessary to distinguish systematically between basilectal and acrolectal variants. However, this is hardly possible because neither basilect nor acrolect are fully stabilized and because of the aforementioned continuum of fluctuating intermediate varieties. In the following sections I have ignored data which are exclusively attested at acrolectal points of the ALR. It should be kept in mind that a presentation not paying sufficient attention to the acrolect is, of course, incomplete.

Nouns and determinants are mostly invariable, but residual marks of number and gender are observed, especially in the case of determiners.

Nouns with agglutinated consonants are frequent, e.g. larzan ‚Äòmoney‚Äô, zwazo ‚Äòbird‚Äô; some of them have different variants, not necessarily depending on the syntactic context in which they occur, e.g. en zanfan / enn anfan ‚Äòa child‚Äô, tire lanfan ‚Äòto abort‚Äô; en lor(e)ye / en zor(e)ye ‚Äòa pillow‚Äô. Syllabic agglutination of the French articles is very rare, e.g. en gro laplui, but also en gros plui ‚Äòa heavy rain‚Äô.

     Morphological distinctions are found to express natural gender for human nouns, e.g. kouzen / kouzin ‚Äòcousin‚Äô, kaf / kafrin ‚Äòperson of African origin‚Äô, malbar / malbarez, malbard ‚ÄòIndian‚Äô; for animals, mal ‚Äòmale‚Äô or femel ‚Äòfemale‚Äô can be used: en mal denn ‚Äòa turkey (cock)‚Äô, enn femel lapen ‚Äòa doe (of rabbit)‚Äô.

     Number marking: Bare nouns can have plural meaning, but plural is mostly marked with le for inanimates and bann for animates, e.g. le ros, le gale ‚Äòthe stones‚Äô, bann zanfan, bann marmay ‚Äòthe children‚Äô. 6.  Verb phrase

About 90% of all verbs in Reunion Creole have two forms, a short form and a long form ending in -e. The short form is used for the present: mi manz ‚ÄòI eat‚Äô and the past: mwen te i manz ‚ÄòI ate‚Äô. In other cases the use of the two forms is ‚Äúgoverned by a context-sensitive rule that deletes the -e when the following element is a constituent governed by the verb‚Äù (Corne 1999: 80), e.g. mwen la manze ‚ÄòI have eaten‚Äô / mwen la manz mon kari. ‚ÄòI have eaten my curry.‚Äô

Some verbs have only one form, e.g. bwar ‚Äòdrink‚Äô, balye ‚Äòsweep‚Äô, and some have long forms roughly corresponding to French infinitives or past participles, e.g. mi par ‚ÄòI leave‚Äô, mwen la parti ‚ÄòI have left‚Äô, mi sar partir ‚ÄòI will leave‚Äô; mi vann ‚ÄòI sell‚Äô, mwen la vann/vandi ‚ÄòI sold‚Äô, the variant vandi being acrolectal. Some verbs show suppletion according to tense and aspect: ale ‚Äòto go‚Äô (anou ale ‚Äòlet‚Äôs go‚Äô, li sava la boutik ‚Äòs/he goes to the shop‚Äô) and the verbs et ‚Äòto be‚Äô (et (infinitive), le (present), (le)te (past), sra (future), sre (conditional)) and avwar ‚Äòto have‚Äô ((na)na / la (present), nave / lave (past), nora / lora (future), nore / lore (conditional)). Avwar expresses transitive possession and is also used as an auxiliary and existential verb. The verb ‚Äòto be necessary‚Äô has the following forms: fo (present), fale (past), fodra (future), fodre (conditional).

     Tense and aspect are expressed by means of preposed markers, e.g. fin (perfect): mwen fin dir aou ‚ÄòI told you‚Äô, by constructions with copula, e.g. apre/antren d (progressive): li l(e) apre / antren d manze ‚Äòs/he is eating‚Äô, or by verbal periphrases, e.g. sort, vyen d (immediate past):

The present tense (covering ongoing or habitual actions and occurring in narratives) is expressed by the short form of the verb, preceded by i, with full or reduced forms of the personal pronoun: m-i sant / mwen i sant ‚ÄòI sing‚Äô. The particle i is not a tense marker; it has been described as ‚Äúindice verbal‚Äù by Cellier (1985: 84) or marker of ‚Äúfiniteness in all persons and numbers‚Äù (Michaelis 2000: 169).

     The future is marked with va (-a with reduced pronouns) or sava, sar, sa, e.g. ma/mi sa(va) rod zerb ‚ÄòI will fetch grass‚Äô. 10.     Lexicon

The count of a sample from Baggioni (1990) shows that more than 95% of the Reunion Creole lexicon is of French origin. As Chaudenson has shown in his pioneering work on Indian Ocean Creoles (1974: 1080–1105), many words of non-French origin reflect the contacts between French settlers and their spouses and servants from Madagascar and India in the first decades of the colony. The role of the women must have been very important. In 1686, half of the women living in Bourbon were Malagasies, and 12 out of 36 French p√®res de familles had Indo-Portuguese wives. Chaudenson identified 95 words of Malagasy origin, about half of them referring to flora and fauna unknown to the French colonists, e.g. fatak ‚Äòtall grass for making brooms‚Äô, vwem ‚Äòcow bean‚Äô, bisik ‚Äòsmall species of fish‚Äô, tang ‚Äòanimal similar to a hedgehog‚Äô, zourit ‚Äòoctopus‚Äô. Several terms for tools, household utensils, food, and drink were introduced by the Malagasies: kalou ‚Äòpestle‚Äô, farfar ‚Äòlarge kitchen rack‚Äô, fangok ‚Äòshort-handled hoe‚Äô, firang ‚Äòharpoon‚Äô, tant ‚Äòbag made of vakwa leaves‚Äô, kanbar ‚Äòyam‚Äô, fangouren ‚Äòsugar-cane wine‚Äô, roumazav ‚ÄòMalagasy dish made of beef, onions, and vegetables‚Äô, soso ‚Äògruel‚Äô, and also some adjectives, all of them expressing unpleasant qualities, e.g. maf ‚Äòoverripe‚Äô, makot ‚Äòdirty, worn out‚Äô, malang ‚Äòfoul-smelling‚Äô, mavouz ‚Äòsick/tired‚Äô. As is well known, Indian Ocean cuisine is of Indian origin, this explains why among the 72 ‚Äútermes indo-portugais‚Äù described by Chaudenson, there are many words denoting vegetables, spices, and dishes, e.g. kari ‚Äòcurry‚Äô, the creole dish par excellence, served with rice, the staple food in Reunion, Mauritius, and the Seychelles. The kari can be accompanied by asar (< Indo-Portuguese achar) ‚Äòpickles‚Äô or rougay (< Tamil ūru-kāy) ‚Äòspicy salad with onions and unripe fruits‚Äô, bred (< Portuguese bredo) ‚Äòplants with edible leaves, cooked in water‚Äô, brenzel (< Indo-Portuguese bringela) ‚Äòeggplant‚Äô, etc. These Indo-Portuguese words are distinguished from the 62 words of Tamil origin, borrowed in the course of the 19th century when indentured workers from India arrived on the island. The use of these terms, many of them referring to religious ceremonies, is restricted to the Indo-Reunionnais community. Words of Amerindian or Spanish origin, e.g. boukane (< Tupi moka√©m) ‚Äòto smoke food‚Äô, bagas (< Spanish bagazo) ‚Äòremains of sugar-cane after the juice has been extracted‚Äô are classified by Chaudenson as ‚Äúvocabulaire des isles‚Äù, transmitted into Reunionnais French and Creole by settlers and administrators who had taken part in the colonization of the Lesser Antilles before coming to Bourbon.
